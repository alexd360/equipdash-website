## 1. Voice & Tone

### Brand Voice: Confident, Clear, Warm

EquipDash should sound like a knowledgeable friend who runs a successful outdoor business -- someone who understands the daily grind and speaks plainly about how to fix it.

### Voice Attributes

| Attribute | What it means | What it does NOT mean |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Confident** | We know our product works and say so directly | Arrogant, pushy, or hyperbolic |
| **Clear** | Short sentences, plain words, no jargon | Dumbed down or condescending |
| **Warm** | Human, relatable, occasionally conversational | Overly casual, slangy, or frivolous |
| **Specific** | Concrete outcomes and real scenarios | Vague promises or hand-waving |
| **Active** | "You manage bookings" not "Bookings are managed" | Passive constructions |

### Tone Spectrum by Context

- **Homepage hero**: Bold, aspirational, direct
- **Feature pages**: Practical, benefit-driven, clear
- **Pricing page**: Transparent, reassuring, straightforward
- **Blog/resources**: Educational, helpful, authoritative
- **Error states/support**: Empathetic, clear, solution-oriented
- **CTA sections**: Energizing, action-oriented, low-friction

### Writing Principles (The "Stripe/Linear" Standard)

1. **Lead with outcomes, not features.** People buy results, not software.
   - BAD: "Our booking management module provides comprehensive scheduling capabilities"
   - GOOD: "Fill every rental slot, even during peak season"

2. **Be specific, not superlative.** Replace "best" with proof.
   - BAD: "The best rental software on the market"
   - GOOD: "Used by 200+ rental businesses to process 50K+ bookings"

3. **Write at a 7th-8th grade reading level.** Use the Hemingway App. Aim for short sentences (15 words average). One idea per sentence.

4. **Use "you" and "your" liberally.** The copy is about the reader, not about EquipDash.
   - BAD: "EquipDash provides an all-in-one platform that..."
   - GOOD: "You get one platform that runs your entire business"

5. **Front-load the benefit.** Put the most important word or phrase at the start of the sentence and heading.
   - BAD: "With EquipDash's advanced features, you can track inventory"
   - GOOD: "Track every piece of gear in real time"

6. **Cut filler words ruthlessly.** Remove: "that", "just", "really", "very", "in order to", "actually", "basically", "simply", "leverage", "utilize".

### Sentence Length Rules

- Headlines: 4-10 words ideal, never exceed 12 words
- Subheadlines: 12-25 words
- Body paragraphs: 2-3 sentences max per paragraph
- Bullet points: 8-15 words each

## 2. Homepage Copy Framework

### The Homepage Job

The homepage has ONE job: get the visitor to self-identify ("this is for me") and take the next step (start trial or book demo). It must answer these questions within 5 seconds:

1. What does this product do?
2. Who is it for?
3. Why should I care?

### Hero Section (Above the Fold)

The hero is the most important 400 pixels on the website. Structure:

```
[Social proof micro-element]     ← e.g., "Trusted by 200+ rental businesses"
[Headline]                       ← Primary value proposition (6-10 words)
[Subheadline]                    ← Supporting detail (1-2 sentences, max 30 words)
[Primary CTA]  [Secondary CTA]  ← "Start Free Trial" + "Book a Demo"
[Risk reversal]                  ← "No credit card required"
[Hero visual]                    ← Product screenshot or demo video
```

**Hero Headline Formula** (choose one per page):

| Formula | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **[Desired outcome] for [audience]** | "More bookings, less busywork for rental operators" |
| **[Verb] your [thing]. [Verb] your [result].** | "Simplify your rentals. Grow your revenue." |
| **The [noun] that [benefit]** | "The rental platform that fills every slot" |
| **[Pain point]? [Solution in 3-5 words].** | "Drowning in spreadsheets? One dashboard. Done." |

**Hero Subheadline Formula:**
State what the product IS, who it's FOR, and what outcome it DELIVERS in one sentence.

Pattern: "[Product] is [category descriptor] for [audience] that [key benefit]."

Example: "EquipDash is the all-in-one platform for equipment rental and tour operators that turns chaos into bookings."

### Section Flow (Below the Fold)

Follow this proven SaaS homepage sequence:

```
1. Hero (value prop + CTA)
2. Trust bar (logos, stats, micro-proof)
3. Problem agitation (2-3 pain points the audience feels)
4. Solution overview (how EquipDash solves it, 3-4 pillars)
5. Who it's for (audience segments with clear matching)
6. Feature highlights (3-4 categories, benefit-first)
7. Social proof (testimonials, case studies, stats)
8. Integrations (logo bar, "works with your tools")
9. FAQ (objection handling disguised as questions)
10. Final CTA (restated value prop + action)
```

### Trust Bar Best Practices

Place immediately below the hero. Options:

- Logo bar: 5-8 customer logos (most recognizable)
- Stats bar: 3-4 proof metrics ("200+ businesses", "50K+ bookings", "98% satisfaction")
- Review badges: G2, Capterra, or industry-specific awards
- Micro-testimonial: One powerful sentence + customer name

## 3. Headline Formulas

### Proven Frameworks

**PAS (Problem - Agitation - Solution):**
Works for any page where the reader has a clear pain point.

1. Problem: Name the pain clearly
2. Agitate: Make them feel the cost of inaction
3. Solution: Present your product as the answer

Example:
- P: "Managing rentals with spreadsheets?"
- A: "Every manual error costs you bookings and reputation."
- S: "EquipDash automates it all from one dashboard."

**AIDA (Attention - Interest - Desire - Action):**
Works for landing pages and feature pages.

1. Attention: Bold headline that stops scrolling
2. Interest: Expand with specific benefit
3. Desire: Show the transformation (before/after)
4. Action: Clear CTA

**BAB (Before - After - Bridge):**
Works for "Who We Are For" pages and case studies.

1. Before: Paint the current painful reality
2. After: Show the desired state
3. Bridge: EquipDash is how you get there

### Headline Types & When to Use Them

| Type | When to use | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Outcome headline** | Homepage hero, landing pages | "Fill every rental slot, even in peak season" |
| **Question headline** | Feature pages, blog | "What if your bookings managed themselves?" |
| **How-to headline** | Feature detail pages, blog | "How to eliminate no-shows with automated reminders" |
| **Number headline** | Blog, comparison pages | "5 ways EquipDash saves you 10 hours per week" |
| **Command headline** | CTA sections, buttons | "Start your free trial" |
| **Curiosity headline** | Blog, email subjects | "The booking mistake costing rental shops $12K/year" |

### Headline Testing Principles

- The headline should be understandable without the subheadline
- Remove articles ("a", "the") where possible in headlines
- Put the keyword near the front for SEO
- Avoid starting with your brand name (the reader does not care yet)

## 4. Feature Page Copy

### Feature Page Architecture

Every feature page follows a consistent structure:

```
1. Hero: Feature name + outcome headline + 1-2 sentence description + CTA
2. Trust bar: Key stats or micro-proof relevant to this feature
3. Feature benefit 1: Image + headline + 2-3 sentence description (image left)
4. Feature benefit 2: Image + headline + 2-3 sentence description (image right)
5. Feature benefit 3: Image + headline + 2-3 sentence description (image left)
6. Related features: Cross-links to complementary features
7. FAQ: Feature-specific objection handling
8. CTA: Feature-specific closing with value reinforcement
```

### Feature Copy Rules

**Rule 1: Benefit-first, feature-second.**
Every section headline states the outcome. The body text can explain the mechanism.

- WRONG: "Inventory Management with Smart Tracking"
- RIGHT: "Know exactly where every piece of gear is, in real time"
- Then in body: "EquipDash tracks availability across locations, sends maintenance alerts, and syncs with your POS automatically."

**Rule 2: One feature per section.**
Do not bundle multiple features into one section. Each deserves its own space with a clear visual.

**Rule 3: Use the "So what?" test.**
After writing each sentence, ask "so what?" If the reader could ask that, you have not gone far enough.

- "EquipDash offers real-time availability updates." (So what?)
- "Customers see exactly what's available and book instantly -- no phone calls, no waiting, no lost sales." (Now we're done.)

**Rule 4: Paint the before and after.**
For each feature benefit section, contrast the old way vs. the EquipDash way.

Pattern:
"Stop [painful old process]. With EquipDash, [new better process] -- [concrete outcome]."

Example:
"Stop chasing down waivers on paper. With EquipDash, customers sign digitally before they arrive -- so your team can focus on the experience, not the paperwork."

**Rule 5: Include one concrete, specific scenario per section.**
Name the industry, the task, the outcome.

Example:
"A ski rental shop in Whistler used to spend 2 hours each morning sorting out yesterday's returns. With EquipDash's inventory tracking, they now start the day with gear already allocated and ready to go."

### Feature Description Length

- Category page (e.g., /features/bookings): 2-3 sentences per sub-feature
- Individual feature page (e.g., /features/bookings/online-booking): 3-5 sentences per benefit section
- Features index page: 1 sentence per category

## 5. Pricing Page Copy

### Pricing Page Goals

1. Build confidence that the pricing is fair and transparent
2. Help visitors self-select the right plan
3. Remove purchase anxiety
4. Drive trial signups

### Pricing Page Structure

```
1. Hero: Clear headline + one sentence about value/transparency
2. Billing toggle: Monthly/Annual with savings callout
3. Plan cards (3 recommended): Name, price, audience fit, key features, CTA
4. Comparison table: Full feature breakdown
5. FAQ: Pricing-specific objections
6. Final CTA: Risk reversal emphasis
```

### Plan Naming Best Practices

Use names that signal growth stages, not arbitrary labels:
- Starter / Growth / Pro (current -- this works well)
- Alternative patterns: Essentials / Professional / Enterprise

### Plan Description Copy

Each plan card needs:

1. **Plan name** (1 word)
2. **Who it's for** (1 sentence, specific): "For growing teams with up to 15 staff"
3. **Price** (prominent, with billing period)
4. **CTA button** (same text on all plans for simplicity)
5. **Risk reversal** (below CTA): "No credit card required"
6. **Feature list** (5-7 items, most impactful first)

### Pricing Copy Rules

- **Show annual savings as a percentage**: "Save 17% with annual billing"
- **Highlight the recommended plan** visually (badge: "Most Popular")
- **Anchor with the middle plan**: The middle plan should be the one most businesses choose
- **Use feature labels, not technical names**: "Smart inventory alerts" not "Automated inventory threshold notifications"
- **Every plan should feel complete**: Never make the cheapest plan feel crippled
- **Add "Everything in [previous plan], plus:"** for the upgrade path clarity

### Pricing FAQ (Must-Have Questions)

1. How does the free trial work?
2. Can I switch plans later?
3. Is there a setup fee?
4. What happens after the trial?
5. Do you offer discounts for annual billing?
6. Can I cancel anytime?

## 6. Call-to-Action (CTA) Copy

### Primary CTAs

| Context | CTA Text | Why it works |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| Homepage hero | "Start Your Free Trial" | Action + value (free) |
| Feature pages | "Try [Feature] Free" | Specific + low commitment |
| Pricing page | "Start Free Trial" | Consistent, low friction |
| Demo page | "Book a Demo" | Clear, specific action |
| Blog posts | "See How It Works" | Curiosity-driven |
| Bottom of page | "Get Started Free" | Action + value |

### Secondary CTAs

| Context | CTA Text | Why it works |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| Next to primary | "Book a Demo" or "Watch Demo" | For prospects not ready to commit |
| Feature pages | "See All Features" | Exploration path |
| Blog | "Read More" | Content progression |

### CTA Copy Rules

1. **Start with a verb.** "Start", "Get", "Try", "See", "Book" -- never "Click here" or "Submit".

2. **Include the value in the button.** "Start Your Free Trial" > "Sign Up" > "Submit".

3. **Match the CTA to the commitment level.**
   - Low commitment: "See How It Works", "Watch Demo"
   - Medium commitment: "Start Free Trial", "Book a Demo"
   - High commitment: "Get Started", "Choose Plan"

4. **Add a risk reversal below the CTA.** Always include one of:
   - "No credit card required"
   - "Cancel anytime"
   - "Free for 21 days"
   - "Setup in under 5 minutes"

5. **Use first person sparingly but strategically.** "Start My Free Trial" can outperform "Start Your Free Trial" in some contexts -- test both.

6. **Never use more than 5 words in a button.**

7. **One primary CTA per viewport.** Too many choices create decision paralysis.

### CTA Section (Bottom-of-Page)

Every page should end with a CTA section. Structure:

```
[Headline]: Restate the value proposition in outcome terms
[1-2 sentences]: Reinforce the benefit or address the final objection
[Primary CTA button]   [Secondary CTA link]
```

**CTA Section Headlines (rotate these):**
- "Ready to simplify your rentals?"
- "See what EquipDash can do for your business"
- "Your next booking is waiting"
- "Start filling more slots today"

## 7. Social Proof

### Types of Social Proof & When to Use Each

| Type | Where to use | Impact level |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Customer logos** | Homepage trust bar, pricing page | High (brand recognition) |
| **Stats/metrics** | Homepage, demo page, feature pages | Very high (concrete proof) |
| **Testimonial quotes** | Feature pages, homepage, pricing | High (emotional) |
| **Case studies** | Dedicated pages, linked from features | Very high (detailed proof) |
| **Review badges** | Homepage, pricing, footer | Medium (third-party validation) |
| **Customer count** | Hero section, trust bar | Medium (bandwagon effect) |
| **Integration logos** | Homepage, features, footer | Medium (ecosystem trust) |

### How to Write Effective Testimonials

When collecting or editing testimonials, aim for the following formula:

**"[Specific result] since [using specific feature]. Before EquipDash, [old painful way]. Now [new better way]."**

Example:
"We've increased bookings by 35% since switching to EquipDash's online booking. Before, we were taking phone reservations on sticky notes. Now customers book themselves 24/7 and we haven't missed a slot in months."

-- Sarah Chen, Owner, Mountain Bike Adventures

### Testimonial Copy Rules

1. **Include specifics**: Numbers, timeframes, feature names
2. **Include the "before"**: The contrast makes the result tangible
3. **Name, title, company**: Full attribution increases trust
4. **Photo if possible**: Human faces increase trust 35%+
5. **Keep under 50 words**: Nobody reads long testimonials
6. **Place near related features**: A booking testimonial near the booking section
7. **Avoid generic praise**: "Great software!" is worthless. "Cut our admin time by 3 hours/day" is powerful.

### Stats/Metrics Bar

Use 3-4 metrics maximum. Format:

```
[Big number] + [What it represents]
```

Current stats to use:
- "200+ active businesses"
- "50K+ bookings processed"
- "98% customer satisfaction"
- "35% average revenue increase"

Rules for stats:
- Round numbers feel more trustworthy (200+, not 213)
- Update quarterly to stay current
- Use "+" to indicate growth
- Bold the number, regular weight for the label

## 8. AI Messaging Guidelines

### The Core Challenge

AI is a powerful differentiator but also a minefield. Overpromising leads to disappointment and churn. Underpromising means missing the competitive advantage.

### AI Positioning Framework

**Position AI as an assistant, not a replacement.**

The message should be: "AI handles the tedious work so you can focus on what matters -- your customers and your business."

### AI Copy Rules

1. **Lead with the outcome, not the technology.**
   - BAD: "Powered by advanced machine learning algorithms"
   - GOOD: "Get pricing recommendations that match demand automatically"

2. **Be concrete about what AI does, not what it "could" do.**
   - BAD: "AI-driven insights that transform your business"
   - GOOD: "AI analyzes your last 6 months of bookings to predict next month's demand"

3. **Avoid these AI buzzwords:**
   - "Revolutionary"
   - "Groundbreaking"
   - "Cutting-edge"
   - "Next-generation"
   - "Powered by AI" (as the headline -- bury the tech, lead with the value)
   - "Leverage AI"
   - "Harness the power of AI"

4. **Use these AI framings instead:**
   - "Smart [feature]" -- "Smart pricing suggestions"
   - "Automated [task]" -- "Automated demand forecasting"
   - "[Action] automatically" -- "Adjust prices automatically based on demand"
   - "AI-assisted [noun]" -- "AI-assisted content creation"

5. **Always pair AI claims with a human control element.**
   - "AI suggests optimal pricing -- you approve changes with one click"
   - "AI drafts product descriptions -- you review and publish"

6. **Include a "how it works" for every AI feature.**
   People trust what they understand. Briefly explain the mechanism:
   - "EquipDash analyzes your booking patterns, local events, and weather data to recommend pricing adjustments. You review suggestions in your dashboard and apply them with one click."

7. **Avoid implying sentience or autonomy.**
   - BAD: "AI that understands your business"
   - GOOD: "Automated insights based on your booking data"

### AI Feature Naming Convention

| Instead of | Use |
|------------|-----|
| AI-Powered Insights | Smart Business Reports |
| AI Dynamic Pricing | Automated Pricing Suggestions |
| AI Content Generation | Auto-Generated Descriptions |
| AI Demand Prediction | Demand Forecasting |
| AI-Driven Analytics | Smart Analytics |

## 9. SEO-Focused Copy

### On-Page SEO Copy Guidelines

**Title Tags (60 characters max):**
Format: `[Primary Keyword] | EquipDash` or `[Primary Keyword] for [Audience] | EquipDash`

Examples:
- "Online Booking Software for Rental Businesses | EquipDash"
- "Equipment Rental Management Platform | EquipDash"
- "Tour Operator Software | EquipDash"

**Meta Descriptions (155 characters max):**
Format: `[What it is] + [key benefit] + [CTA phrase]`

Example:
- "EquipDash helps rental and tour operators manage bookings, inventory, and customers from one platform. Start your free 21-day trial."

**H1 Tags:**
- One per page, always
- Include primary keyword naturally
- Should read as a complete, compelling statement
- Do NOT stuff with keywords

**Header Hierarchy (H2-H4):**
- H2: Section headings (include secondary keywords naturally)
- H3: Sub-section headings
- H4: Detail headings within sections
- Never skip levels (H1 -> H3 without H2)

### Keyword Integration Rules

1. **Primary keyword**: In H1, first paragraph, title tag, meta description, URL slug
2. **Secondary keywords**: In H2s and body copy, naturally distributed
3. **Long-tail phrases**: In FAQ questions and blog content
4. **Never keyword stuff**: If it reads unnaturally, rewrite it

### SEO + User Experience Balance

The copy must serve the reader FIRST and search engines SECOND. A useful heuristic:

- Write the copy for humans first
- Review and ensure primary keywords appear naturally
- Add secondary keywords only where they do not disrupt readability
- FAQ sections are excellent for capturing long-tail search queries

### URL Structure

- Use lowercase, hyphen-separated slugs
- Include primary keyword in the URL
- Keep URLs short: `/features/online-booking` not `/features/bookings/online-booking-system-for-rental-businesses`

## 10. Site Structure & Page Hierarchy

### Recommended Site Map

A B2B SaaS site should have 4 levels maximum. The ideal structure for EquipDash:

```
Level 0: Homepage

Level 1 (Main Nav):
  - Features (overview)
  - Who We're For (overview)
  - Pricing
  - Blog
  - Book a Demo / Start Trial

Level 2 (Feature Categories):
  - /features/bookings
  - /features/customer-experience
  - /features/resource-management
  - /features/planning
  - /features/operations
  - /features/ai-automation
  - /features/integrations

Level 2 (Audience Segments):
  - /who-we-are-for/equipment-rental-providers
  - /who-we-are-for/experience-tour-operators

Level 3 (Individual Features):
  - /features/bookings/online-booking
  - /features/bookings/booking-management
  - etc.

Level 3 (Industry Verticals):
  - /who-we-are-for/ski-snowboard
  - /who-we-are-for/bikes
  - etc.

Utility Pages:
  - /contact
  - /privacy-policy
  - /terms-of-use
  - /help
```

### Page Count Guidelines

For a B2B SaaS at EquipDash's stage:

- **Essential pages** (must-have): Homepage, Features overview, 2-3 feature category pages, Pricing, Book a Demo, Contact, About/Company, Privacy, Terms = ~10-12 pages
- **Growth pages** (build as content matures): Individual feature pages, industry vertical pages, blog posts, case studies, comparison pages = 20-50 pages
- **Scale pages** (long-term SEO play): Blog posts, directory, landing pages for campaigns = 50-200+ pages

### Navigation Copy

- Keep nav labels to 1-2 words: "Features", "Pricing", "Blog"
- Avoid jargon in nav: "Solutions" is vague, "Features" is clear
- CTA button in nav should stand out visually: "Start Free Trial"
- Mega menus should use descriptive labels, not just feature names:
  - "Online Booking" with subtitle "Let customers book 24/7"

## 11. Copy Patterns to Avoid

### Anti-Patterns Found in Current Copy

These are specific patterns identified in the existing EquipDash copy that should be revised:

1. **Exclamation point overuse.**
   Current: "Unleash the Power of EquipDash--Every Feature You Need, One Platform!"
   Better: "Every feature you need. One platform."
   Rule: Maximum 1 exclamation point per page, and only in CTA sections.

2. **Starting headlines with the brand name.**
   Current: "EquipDash Online Booking Delivers!"
   Better: "Fill every rental slot, 24/7"
   Rule: Lead with the benefit. The brand name belongs in the logo, not the headline.

3. **Rhetorical pain questions in hero subheadlines.**
   Current: "Tired of juggling clunky software, missing bookings, and manual headaches?"
   Better: State the solution directly. Pain questions work in body copy, not in hero intros.
   Rule: Hero subheadline should ANSWER what the product does, not ask if the reader has problems.

4. **Lorem ipsum / placeholder text left in production.**
   Current: Multiple instances of "Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet..."
   Rule: Never ship placeholder copy. If copy is not ready, use "[COPY NEEDED: description of what goes here]".

5. **Superlative claims without evidence.**
   Current: "#1 choice for rental pros and tour operators worldwide"
   Better: "Trusted by 200+ rental and tour businesses" (with actual data)
   Rule: Every claim needs backing. If you cannot prove it, do not say it.

6. **Repetitive CTA language.**
   Current: Nearly every page ends with "Don't let outdated software hold you back."
   Better: Vary the CTA section copy to match the page context.
   Rule: Each page's closing CTA should reference the specific value discussed on that page.

7. **Feature-focused instead of benefit-focused headings.**
   Current: "Block for Each Category" (unclear meaning)
   Better: "Every tool your rental business needs, organized by what matters"
   Rule: Every heading should pass the "would a busy operator care?" test.

8. **Run-on subheadlines.**
   Current: "Tired of manual guesswork, stagnant growth, and software that can't innovate your rentals or tours? EquipDash's AI & Automations features--insights, pricing, and content--supercharge efficiency, boost bookings, and save time."
   Better: Two shorter sentences. One about the problem, one about the solution.
   Rule: Subheadlines should be scannable. Max 30 words.

9. **Inconsistent copy descriptions (swapped descriptions).**
   Current: "Asset Maintenance" has the description "Gain actionable insights to optimize revenue and efficiency" (this is the Reports description).
   Rule: Audit all feature descriptions to ensure accuracy.

10. **Overuse of em-dashes as copy crutches.**
    Current: Heavy use of "--" to connect clauses.
    Better: Use periods. Make two sentences. Em-dashes should be rare stylistic choices, not structural.

## 12. EquipDash-Specific Terminology

### Product Vocabulary

Use these terms consistently across all pages:

| Concept | Use | Don't use |
|---------|-----|-----------|
| The product | EquipDash | "our platform", "the system", "our software" (occasionally OK) |
| Free trial | "Free 21-day trial" | "Free trial" (always specify duration) |
| Target audience | "Rental and tour operators" | "Users", "clients", "customers" (when referring to EquipDash buyers) |
| Their customers | "Your customers", "guests", "renters" | "End users" |
| Signing up | "Start your free trial" | "Register", "Sign up", "Create account" |
| Dashboard | "Your dashboard" | "Admin panel", "backend", "control panel" |
| AI features | "Smart [feature name]" | "AI-powered", "AI-driven" (overused) |
| Support | "Dedicated support team" | "Customer service", "help desk" |

### Industry Verticals

Always use the customer's language:

| Vertical | Their language | Not this |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| Ski/Snowboard | "Gear", "rental shop", "slopes", "season" | "Equipment units", "retail outlet" |
| Bikes | "Fleet", "rides", "bike shop" | "Vehicle inventory" |
| Water Sports | "Boards", "gear", "on the water" | "Aquatic equipment" |
| Tours | "Experiences", "trips", "guides" | "Tour products" |
| Boats/Charters | "Vessels", "trips", "captains" | "Watercraft inventory" |

### Seasonal Language

EquipDash customers are often seasonal businesses. Use language that acknowledges this:

- "Peak season" and "off-season" (not "busy period" or "quiet time")
- "Seasonal demand" (not "fluctuating usage")
- "Scale up and down with your season" (not "flexible capacity")

## 13. Page-by-Page Copywriting Checklist

### For Every Page

- [ ] H1 is benefit-focused and contains primary keyword
- [ ] Title tag is under 60 characters and includes brand name
- [ ] Meta description is under 155 characters with a CTA phrase
- [ ] Hero section answers: What is it? Who is it for? Why care?
- [ ] At most 1 exclamation point on the entire page
- [ ] Every heading passes the "so what?" test
- [ ] CTA buttons use action verbs (Start, Get, Try, Book, See)
- [ ] Risk reversal appears near every CTA ("No credit card required")
- [ ] Social proof appears within 1 scroll of the hero
- [ ] No placeholder/lorem ipsum text
- [ ] No unsupported superlative claims
- [ ] Copy reads naturally at 7th-8th grade level
- [ ] Page ends with a contextual CTA section

### Homepage Specific

- [ ] Hero headline is 6-10 words, benefit-first
- [ ] Hero subheadline explains WHAT + WHO + WHY in under 30 words
- [ ] Trust bar with quantified proof (not just "trusted by SMBs")
- [ ] "Who we're for" section helps visitors self-identify
- [ ] Feature overview uses outcomes, not feature names
